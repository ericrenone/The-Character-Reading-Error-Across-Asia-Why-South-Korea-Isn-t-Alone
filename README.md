**The Asian Lookism Hierarchy (by regulatory weakness and market expansion):**

## Part One: China's Hidden Epidemic

### The Scale of Lookism in Chinese Employment

Research on lookism in Chinese employment decisions shows discrimination against applicants who attached unattractive photos was more pronounced for interview decisions when applicants had low qualifications. Both male and female raters' ratings of interview, hiring, and salary decisions decreased as attractiveness decreased, with more pronounced effects for male raters. Studies show discrimination is especially prevalent in roles occupied by women, and that attractive applicants have higher callback rates than unattractive ones with identical qualifications.

Evidence from promotion studies of Chinese municipal leaders found that leaders with greater perceived attractiveness have higher probability of promotion, with no correlation between a leader's facial traits and local economic growth under their jurisdiction. This suggests appearance-based discrimination exists when Chinese political hierarchy screens political elites.

**Why China is more extreme than South Korea:**
- No legal ban on appearance-based discrimination (South Korea banned in 1995, though unenforced)
- Minimal regulatory response to employment discrimination
- Government agencies do not publicly address lookism (unlike South Korea's Ministry occasionally acknowledging the issue)
- Political promotion is openly appearance-based (no pretense of meritocracy)

### China's Cosmetic Surgery Market: The Unregulated Explosion

China's cosmetic surgery market reached 200 billion yuan in 2021 and is expected to reach 400 billion yuan by 2025 and 1.3 trillion yuan ($192.4 billion) by 2030 according to Citic Securities. About 1.05 million people in China underwent plastic surgeries in 2022, with majority being females under age 30. Non-invasive procedures comprise 64.8% of market share in 2025 due to quick results and minimal recovery time for working professionals. Guangdong dominance reflects Greater Bay Area wealth concentration and Hong Kong medical tourism flows.

**The critical difference from South Korea:** South Korea's market is ~3.40B USD (2026) with strong regulatory oversight. China's market is estimated at $192.4B by 2030 with minimal oversight. The vast difference suggests:

- **Unregistered clinics:** Estimated 50%+ of procedures occur in non-regulated facilities
- **Unqualified practitioners:** Dentists, beauticians performing procedures (undocumented)
- **Ghost surgeries:** No official record or follow-up
- **Complication data:** Largely unreported

### The Colorism-Lookism Intersection in China

Beyond facial structure, China experiences **colorism** (skin tone discrimination) layered on top of lookism:
- Light skin is valued (associated with class, indoor work, privilege)
- Darker skin is devalued (associated with outdoor labor, lower class)
- Skin whitening products dominate the market
- Job postings explicitly require "fair complexioned" applicants

This adds a **racial/ethnic discrimination layer** not as prominent in Japan or South Korea.

## Part Two: Japan's Aging-Population Model

### The Different Drivers

Japan's cosmetic surgery market is driven by **different factors** than China and South Korea:

**South Korea/China drivers:** Youthful population pursuing enhancement for dating/career  
**Japan drivers:** Aging population (70%+ over 65 by 2040) pursuing anti-aging

Japan is the third largest cosmetic surgery market in the world and second in Asia-Pacific. Approximately 90% of cosmetic surgeries performed in Japan in 2017 were facial procedures. Key drivers include aging population seeking anti-aging treatments, rising interest in aesthetic wellness among men in their 20s-30s, and medical tourism from China. The market shows shift toward non-invasive procedures (Botox, dermal fillers, laser skin resurfacing) over invasive surgeries, with growth rates of 9.4-15.1% CAGR through 2033.

Analysis of 195,893 double-eyelid embedding procedures in Japan from October 2020 to December 2024 showed peak demand in March 2023 with marked seasonality. Primary demographic comprised women in their 20s, with high demand among students and young office workers, alongside growing male interest.

**Unique aspects of South Korean lookism:**

1. **Legal prohibition (1995) without enforcement:** Most countries lack even a law; South Korea has one but doesn't enforce it

2. **Algorithmic hiring ban (January 2026):** Most aggressive regulatory action on appearance discrimination in Asia-Pacific

5. **Medical tourism dominance:** 45% of South Korean surgery revenue from international patients

6. **Institutional transparency:** Government agencies sometimes acknowledge lookism (unlike China); regulatory framework exists (unlike most of Southeast Asia)

**The South Korea-China contrast:**

- **China:** No legal prohibition; government agencies reinforce appearance-based selection; unregulated market; fastest growth; hidden complications
- **South Korea:** Legal prohibition (unenforced); government acknowledges lookism; regulated market; mature market; visible complications

**The South Korea-Japan contrast:**

- **Japan:** Older regulatory framework; aging-driven market; influencer-normalized (not industry-mandated); cultural resistance declining
- **South Korea:** Younger, more aggressive market; youth-driven appearance optimization; K-pop industry mandates surgery; regulatory response emerging

## Part Four: Southeast Asia's Medical Tourism Boom

### The Cost Arbitrage Model Driving Growth

Thailand leads Southeast Asia in medical tourism with over a million medical tourists annually. Cosmetic surgery procedures can be performed at savings reaching up to 60% compared with Western healthcare systems. Thailand has 60+ Joint Commission International-accredited healthcare facilities as of 2026, reinforcing global reputation for regulated standards. The Thailand medical tourism industry continues to lead ASEAN's health and beauty sector with market revenue reaching USD 24.4 billion in 2023 and projected CAGR of 13.3% from 2024-2032. Thailand ranks 21st globally with over 112,000 cosmetic surgeries performed.

The Southeast Asia medical tourism market reached USD 6.1 billion in 2025 and is forecast to expand to USD 35.5 billion by 2034, representing 21.68% CAGR between 2026-2034—one of the fastest growth trajectories within global healthcare services.

### The Regional Hierarchy

**Thailand:** Established leader, 60 JCI-accredited facilities, 112,000+ procedures/year, government support (2026 "Golden Year of Healthcare")

**Malaysia:** Emerging through MYMT 2026 campaign, targeting RM12 billion by 2026, Flagship Medical Tourism Hospital Programme, positioned for premium market

**Vietnam:** Rapid development, 50-70% cost savings vs Western countries, growing quality, emerging from low-cost reputation

**Indonesia:** New medical tourism special economic zone in Bali (June 2025), strong potential but needs accreditation/language improvements

**Singapore:** Quality/innovation focus, fewer volume procedures, premium pricing, regulatory leadership

### Why Southeast Asia Is Growing Faster Than East Asia

**Market dynamics:**

1. **Cost arbitrage:** 50-70% cheaper than Western countries drives international patients from US, Europe, Australia

2. **Medical tourism integration:** Combining procedures with luxury travel/wellness experiences creates high-value customer

3. **Government support:** Thailand/Malaysia have explicit medical tourism strategies; China/Japan do not

4. **Younger population:** Unlike Japan (aging), Southeast Asia has working-age population seeking cosmetic enhancement

5. **Emerging affluence:** Growing middle class in Thailand, Vietnam, Indonesia creating domestic demand

6. **Regulatory gaps:** Less oversight = faster expansion, easier entry for practitioners

## Conclusion: The Asian Lookism Continuum (Not a Binary)

South Korea is **not** uniquely afflicted by appearance discrimination. It is **maximally advanced along the same continuum** that affects all of Asia.

**The continuum:**

**Left end (minimal impact):** EU countries with strong regulations and enforcement

**Middle (significant impact):** Japan, most developed nations with legal frameworks but cultural preference for appearance

**Right end (extreme impact):** China, South Korea, unregulated Southeast Asia with minimal legal protection and high cultural normalization

**The tragic irony:** South Korea's regulatory response (algorithmic ban, January 2026) may make it appear "more progressive" than China, Japan, or Southeast Asia. But the underlying problem—the col/ker error embedded neurologically, culturally, institutionally, and algorithmically—operates across all of them.

**By 2030:**

- **China** will have fastest market growth (11.8% CAGR) with minimal regulatory improvement
- **South Korea** will have most visible mental health crisis (documented body dysmorphia epidemic)
- **Japan** will have matured market with aging-population focus
- **Southeast Asia** will have largest absolute growth (medical tourism $35.5B by 2034) with largest regulatory gaps
- **All of them** will maintain appearance-based discrimination at roughly current levels, just in less visible forms

The difference is not whether lookism exists. The difference is **visibility**: what is measured, acknowledged, regulated, and treated versus what remains hidden.

## Part Nine: The Regulatory Gap Widening (2026-2030)

### The Paradox

As cosmetic surgery regulation increases in developed Asian markets (South Korea, Japan, Singapore), it **decreases** in growth markets (SE Asia). This creates:

1. **Regulatory arbitrage:** Patients travel from regulated markets to unregulated ones
2. **Quality divergence:** Premium clinics in Korea/Japan vs. variable-quality clinics in SE Asia
3. **Complication patterns:** High-end complications in developed markets, basic infections/asymmetries in SE Asia
4. **International flow:** Wealthy patients go to Korea/Japan; middle-income patients go to Thailand/Vietnam
